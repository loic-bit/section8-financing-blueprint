The 5 strategies

Pick your path

Every strategy below has closed real deals with real students. None of them are theory.

01
Best for: Stable W2 · 720+ Credit
Classic 20% Conventional Loan

The most straightforward investment loan. You put 20% down, get the best interest rates on the market, and keep things simple. The catch: stricter requirements and you max out at 10 loans.

Requirements
  • 720+ credit score
  • Stable W2 income (2+ years tax returns)
  • Low debt-to-income ratio (under 40%)
  • 20% down payment
  • $3,500 + 2% of purchase price in closing costs
Pros & Cons
  • Lowest interest rates (~7%)
  • No prepayment penalty on most
  • Simple qualification
  • Cannot close under LLC until 6-12 months seasoning
  • Maxes out at 10 loans total
  • Harder to find lenders for sub-$100K homes
Joseph's Take

This is the cleanest loan if you have a strong W2 and great credit. The LLC restriction is annoying but workable. If you plan to scale past 10 doors, you will need to layer in DSCR loans anyway.

02
Best for: Self-Employed · LLC Buyers · No Income Verification
Classic 20% DSCR Loan

DSCR stands for Debt Service Coverage Ratio. Instead of verifying your personal income, the lender looks at whether the property's rental income covers the mortgage. This is the loan 80% of my students use.

Requirements
  • 680+ credit score
  • 20% down (15% if you already own 1 rental)
  • No W2 or income verification needed
  • Property must appraise
  • Can close under LLC from day one
Pros & Cons
  • Close under LLC immediately
  • Fast closings
  • Works for foreign nationals (35% down)
  • No income limits or W2 required
  • Interest rate 0.5-1% higher than conventional
  • Still needs appraisal
  • Slightly higher fees
Joseph's Take

This is my #1 recommendation for most investors. The slightly higher rate is worth the flexibility, especially if you are self-employed, have multiple income streams, or want asset protection through an LLC from day one. Pair this with a turnkey property and you have the cleanest path to cash flow.

03
Best for: First-Timers · House Hackers · Limited Cash
3% Conventional or 3.5% FHA

The lowest barrier to entry. You live in the property for at least 1 year, then convert it to a rental. Perfect for house hacking a multifamily or getting your first property with minimal cash.

Requirements
  • 580+ credit (FHA) or 620+ (Conventional 3%)
  • Must live in property minimum 1 year
  • Stable income (2 years)
  • Low debt-to-income ratio
Pros & Cons
  • Lowest down payment (3-3.5%)
  • Great for house hacking multifamily
  • Accessible for first-timers
  • Must live there 1 year minimum
  • Mortgage insurance required (0.5-1.5% annually)
  • FHA has strict property condition requirements
Joseph's Take

If you have under $15K and a stable job, this is your entry point. Buy a duplex, live in one unit, rent the other to a Section 8 tenant. After 1 year, move out and repeat.

04
Best for: Entrepreneurs · Fast Scalers · 0% Interest Window
Business Credit Stacking into BRRRR

Stack multiple 0% business credit cards through an LLC to fund a BRRRR deal with virtually no cash out of pocket. High risk, high reward. Only viable if you can execute the rehab and refinance within 12-18 months.

Requirements
  • Established LLC
  • Good personal credit (700+)
  • Ability to manage rehab timeline
  • Must refinance before 0% period ends
Pros & Cons
  • Can start with $0 cash (realistically $10-12K)
  • 0% interest for 12-18 months
  • Can do multiple BRRRRs simultaneously
  • 10-12% lender fees on credit obtained
  • Extremely risky if rehab goes over budget or timeline
  • Requires strong credit and LLC history
Joseph's Take

One of my top recommended strategies for people who want to scale fast. The BRRRR method lets you recycle your capital, meaning you can do deal after deal without needing a full down payment each time. Only attempt this if you have reliable contractors who hit their timelines.

05
Best for: Rehabbers · Experienced Investors · Portfolio Scaling
Hard Money / Private Money into BRRRR

Use a private money lender at 12-13% interest to buy a distressed property, rehab it, get it appraised, then refinance into a DSCR loan, pulling out 75-80% of the appraised value to recycle your capital.

Requirements
  • 620+ credit score
  • ~$15-17K for down payment and renovations
  • Access to reliable contractors
  • Understanding of ARV (after-repair value)
  • Ability to manage a 4-month rehab timeline
Pros & Cons
  • Recycle capital for next deal
  • Build equity fast through forced appreciation
  • One of the most efficient paths to scaling
  • 12-13% interest during rehab period
  • $4,500-5,000 in lender closing fees
  • Risky if ARV comes in lower than expected
  • Requires part-time management during rehab
Joseph's Take

One of my top recommended strategies and how most of my students scale past their first property. Our average BRRRR leaves $10-17K in the deal, roughly half the cost of a turnkey purchase. The key is having vetted contractors who hit their timelines.

Straight talk

What other gurus won't tell you

The Section 8 space is full of misleading claims. Here is the truth.

What They Claim The Reality
"30% ROI guaranteed" Realistic ROI is 15-20% with proper analysis
"Seller financing is the best strategy" Seller financing rarely works in 2025. Terms are terrible for buyers in a high-rate environment
"Subject-To deals are everywhere" Subject-To deals are extremely rare and usually require large cash to the table
"Cash flow is $800+/month" Realistic net cash flow after all expenses is $350-500/month
"Turnkey homes need zero maintenance" Even turnkey homes typically need $2,000-3,000 at move-in to pass Section 8 inspection
"HUD rates are what you can charge" HUD FMR rates are the maximum the government will pay. Actual market rents are often lower
